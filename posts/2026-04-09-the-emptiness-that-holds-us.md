You think you live somewhere. You don't. You live in nothing, surrounded by nothing, hurtling through nothing at speeds you can't feel and distances you can't imagine. And nobody talks about this because it's terrifying. So let me.

![A vast nebula in deep space — billions of stars in a cosmic cloud of gas and dust](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1462331940025-496dfbfc7564?w=1200)

## Stop Calling It "Space"

We say "space" like it's a place. Like it's a room with walls somewhere out past the sky. It's not. Space is the absence of anything. It's not a container. It's the default state of reality. Everything you've ever touched, everyone you've ever known, every war fought, every city built — all of it exists in a hiccup of matter so small the universe doesn't notice.

The observable universe stretches about 93 billion light-years across. Light — the fastest thing there is — would take 93 billion years to cross it. And that's just the part we can see. The rest? We don't know. Could be infinite. Probably is.

Now place our solar system inside that.

## The Oort Cloud: The Edge That Isn't

Most people think the solar system ends at Pluto. It doesn't. Not even close.

Beyond Neptune, beyond the Kuiper Belt, there's a structure called the Oort Cloud. It's a vast, spherical shell of icy bodies that surrounds our entire solar system. It stretches from roughly 2,000 astronomical units out to maybe 100,000 AU. One AU is the distance from the Earth to the Sun — about 150 million kilometers. So the Oort Cloud's outer edge is roughly 100,000 times that distance.

In light-years, the Oort Cloud extends to about 1.6 light-years from the Sun. That makes our full solar system — core to edge — roughly 3.2 light-years across.

Sounds big. It's not.

## Do the Math. Then Sit Down.

The observable universe is 93 billion light-years wide. Our solar system, Oort Cloud and all, is about 3.2 light-years. That means you could line up roughly **29 billion solar systems** end to end across the observable universe.

But that's one dimension. Space is three-dimensional. In volume, the observable universe could contain something on the order of **10^31** solar systems. That's a 1 followed by 31 zeros. Written out, it looks like this:

10,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000

And most of that volume contains absolutely nothing.

The Oort Cloud itself — this supposedly enormous shell — is almost entirely empty. It holds maybe a few trillion objects scattered across a sphere so large that the average distance between any two of them is tens of millions of kilometers. You could fly through it for years and never see a single piece of ice.

That's our backyard. That's the "full extent" of our Sun's gravitational reach. And it's already more empty than full.

## The Gap Between Stars

Here's what really gets me.

The nearest star to our Sun is Proxima Centauri, about 4.24 light-years away. Our Oort Cloud extends to 1.6 light-years. So between the edge of our solar system and the nearest neighbor, there's a gap of roughly 2.6 light-years of... nothing. No star. No planet. No dust worth mentioning. Just void.

And Proxima Centauri is the *closest* one. The average distance between stars in our galaxy is about 5 light-years. In the space between galaxies, you're looking at millions of light-years of emptiness.

The Milky Way has something like 200 billion stars. The observable universe has around 2 trillion galaxies. And between all of them — between every bright dot you see and every one you don't — is nothing. The universe is, conservatively, 99.9999999999999999999% empty.

## Scale It Down, It Gets Worse

People love scale analogies. Fine. Here's one.

Shrink the Sun to a basketball. Earth becomes a peppercorn, about 26 meters away. Jupiter is a walnut at 135 meters. Pluto? A pinhead, 1 kilometer out. The Oort Cloud's outer edge is now about 7 to 10 kilometers from the basketball.

In this model, the nearest star is another basketball sitting roughly 7,000 kilometers away. That's the distance from Sydney to London.

One basketball in Sydney. One in London. Everything in between is empty. And we're worried about what happens on the peppercorn.

## So What?

I'm not saying this to make you feel small. You already are small. Everyone is. Everything is.

I'm saying this because people talk about the universe like it's full of stuff. Like it's crowded with galaxies and nebulas and planets waiting to be explored. It's not. It's a cathedral of absence. The stuff — the stars, the planets, the Oort Cloud debris — is the rounding error. The real universe is the emptiness between things.

And we live in the rounding error of the rounding error.

Our solar system, this thing we treat as a vast territory to explore, is a microscopic puddle in an ocean that stretches beyond comprehension. The Oort Cloud, the farthest boundary of our Sun's influence, is a fence around a yard so small that no cosmic neighbor would ever notice it.

## Why I Won't Shut Up About This

Because the lie we tell ourselves is dangerous. The lie that we're at the center of something. That our planet is big. That the solar system is vast. That we matter on any scale the universe would recognize.

We don't. And that's not sad. That's freeing.

If the universe doesn't care, then there's no cosmic scorekeeper. No predetermined meaning. No grade at the end. The emptiness isn't hostile. It's indifferent. And indifference, if you're brave enough to face it, is the most liberating thing there is.

You don't owe the void an explanation for existing. The void didn't ask you to be here. You showed up anyway — a brief arrangement of matter that can think about its own insignificance. That's not nothing. That's the most improbable thing in 10^31 solar systems worth of space.

So stop flinching at the dark. Look at it. The emptiness that holds us is not our enemy.

It's just honest.
