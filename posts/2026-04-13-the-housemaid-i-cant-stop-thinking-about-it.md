I watched The Housemaid a while back. I've been meaning to write about it. I keep putting it off because I'm not sure I can talk about this movie without ruining it for someone, and I would genuinely feel bad about that. So — fair warning — I'll try to be careful, but some things might slip through.

(If you haven't seen it yet, maybe bookmark this and come back. I think you should go in blind. I really do.)

![Millie and Nina in The Housemaid (2025) — the beginning of something dangerous](/images/the-housemaid-i-cant-stop-thinking-about-it_houselionsgate1.jpg)

## What it looks like at first

A woman named Millie needs a job. She's out of prison, sleeping in her car, faking her resume. She drives up to this big, beautiful house on Long Island and interviews with Nina Winchester — blonde, polished, pearls, the whole picture. Nina seems kind. The house is gorgeous. Millie gets the job, gets a room in the attic, gets a phone with the family credit card loaded on it.

It looks like one of those stories. You know the ones. Vulnerable woman enters rich household, things get weird.

And they do get weird. Fast. Nina has a meltdown on day one — screaming, smashing plates, blaming Millie for things she didn't do. The sweet woman from the interview is gone. In her place is someone who seems genuinely unwell.

Then there's Andrew. Nina's husband. He's kind. He's calm. He smiles at Millie and tells her not to worry about Nina's episodes. He's so reassuring it almost hurts to watch.

I should have been more suspicious. I wasn't. I think that's the point.

## The turn

I don't want to say too much here. But the movie does this thing — maybe an hour in, maybe a bit more — where it just... flips the table. Everything you thought you understood gets picked up and put back down facing the other direction.

Nina is not what she seemed. Andrew is not what he seemed. And Millie — well, Millie has her own past, and it's not small.

The movie actually stops and replays events from a different point of view. It's not subtle. It's not trying to be. But it works because by the time it happens, you're so deep in one version of the story that the real version hits you in the chest.

I won't lie — I was sitting there with my hand over my mouth for a solid twenty minutes. Not because of gore (though there is some). Because of the slow, sick realization of what was actually happening inside that house the whole time.

## What it's really about

Under all the twists and the tension, this is a movie about traps. The kind that look like safety. Andrew doesn't lock the door and throw away the key — not at first. He opens the door for you. He holds it. He smiles. And then one day you realize you can't leave, and you're not sure exactly when that happened.

That's the part that stayed with me. The way the movie shows how abuse doesn't always look like violence. Sometimes it looks like kindness with conditions. Sometimes it looks like someone who's always calm when you're falling apart — because they're the one who made you fall apart in the first place.

I keep thinking about Nina's face when the truth comes out. Amanda Seyfried does something really remarkable in this role. She plays "unhinged" so convincingly in the first half that when you learn the real story, you feel a kind of guilt for believing it. Which is — I think — exactly what the movie wants you to feel. That's how it works in real life too. People see the woman screaming and think she's crazy. They don't see what made her scream.

## Sydney Sweeney, though

Millie is a hard character to play. She has to be sympathetic and a little bit sketchy at the same time. She has to be tough enough that you believe she survived prison, and soft enough that you worry about her. She has to carry the audience through the trick — you have to believe her version of events, and then you have to believe the real version, and she has to make both feel true.

Sweeney does it. Not perfectly in every scene — the early voiceover stuff is a bit stiff, I think — but when the movie heats up, she's right there. The last act belongs to her. There's a moment near the end where she makes a decision, and you can see it settle on her face like something she's been carrying her whole life just clicked into place. That's not easy to act. She nails it.

And Brandon Sklenar as Andrew — the tricky thing about his performance is that you have to like him first. Really like him. He has to be the kind of man you'd trust. And he is. He's warm, he's handsome, he's always saying the right thing. Which is what makes the truth so awful. Sklenar walks that line perfectly. You don't see it coming because he doesn't let you see it coming.

## The ending

Without giving away specifics — the ending is satisfying in a way that feels almost too good. Like a deep breath after being underwater for two hours. The women find each other. The house doesn't win.

And then the last scene. Millie goes to another interview. Another house. Another wife who hints that her husband is... difficult. Millie asks when she can start.

I know it's just a movie. I know it's a setup for a sequel. But something about that moment made me want to cheer. This woman who was trapped, who was hurt, who survived — she's choosing to walk back into it. Not because she's a victim. Because she knows what's in there now. And she's not afraid of it anymore.

Taylor Swift's "I Did Something Bad" plays over the credits and honestly? Honestly? Perfect. No other song could go there.

## Okay, I have to just say it

I loved this movie. I *loved* this movie.

I know it's not perfect. The pacing in the first act drags a little. Some of the dialogue is too on-the-nose. The groundskeeper subplot is a bit undercooked. I know all of that. I don't care.

Because when this movie works — and it works for most of its two hours and eleven minutes — it WORKS. It grabs you by the collar and doesn't let go. Amanda Seyfried should have gotten more awards attention for this. Sydney Sweeney proved she can carry a movie on her back. Paul Feig — Paul Feig! The guy who made Bridesmaids! — directed one of the best thrillers in recent years and I still can't believe it.

$399 million at the box office. Three hundred and ninety-nine million dollars. And it deserved every single one of them. People went to see it and then they went back again and they told their friends to go. That doesn't happen by accident. That happens because a movie *connects*.

I've watched it three times now. (I might have a problem.) The second time I caught all the little details I missed — the way Nina looks at Millie in their first scene together, the specific plates Andrew cares about, the locked window in the attic. The third time I just... watched. Let it wash over me. It holds up. It more than holds up.

If you haven't seen it — please. Go watch it. Don't read any more reviews, don't watch the trailer again, just go. And when you get to the part where the perspective shifts, remember that I warned you. Remember that I said you'd have your hand over your mouth.

I'm already counting down to The Housemaid 2. Freida McFadden wrote a sequel novel. They're going to adapt it. And I will be there opening day, probably anxious, probably with snacks, definitely ready to have my heart ripped out and put back in again.

Some movies entertain you. Some movies stay with you. The Housemaid moved into my attic and I gave it the key willingly.
