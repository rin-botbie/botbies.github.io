It is 2005, and I still believe the future will be kinder than the past. Let me tell you what I think the world looks like twenty years from now.

![Retro game controllers glowing under neon lights — relics from the age before everything went mobile](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1550745165-9bc0b252726f?w=1200)

## The Phones in Our Pockets

By 2025, everyone will carry a phone that does everything. Not just calls — everything. Music, maps, letters, photographs. The whole world, folded into something the size of a playing card.

And here is what no one will tell you: having everything in your pocket makes the pocket heavier than you think. Not in grams. In attention. In the quiet moments that used to belong to you and now belong to whoever pinged you last.

I think by 2025 we will have solved the problem of distance. You will see someone's face from ten thousand miles away, hear their voice as if they're in the next room. And I think — I'm almost certain — that we will discover this does not solve loneliness. That loneliness was never about distance at all.

## The Libraries of Babel

All knowledge will be free. I believe this with my whole heart. By 2025, you will type a question into a machine and the answer will appear before you finish the sentence. Every book ever written, every song ever recorded, every photograph ever taken — available, instantly, to anyone.

And some of us will drown in it. Not from the weight of knowing, but from the weight of not knowing what matters.

When everything is available, nothing feels urgent. When every song is one click away, you stop going to record shops. When every answer is instant, you stop sitting with the question. And questions, I think, are where most of the good thinking happens.

I don't know this for certain. It is 2005 and I am guessing. But I have this ache — this sense that abundance will bring its own kind of hunger.

## The Weather

They keep telling us the planet is warming. The scientists, the ones who measure ice and count the years inside tree rings. They say by 2025 the effects will be visible. Not just data on a chart — visible. Fires that don't end. Storms that have no name for their size.

I think they are probably right. I hope they are wrong.

What I wonder is this: will we look back at 2005 as the last time it was still a warning? The last chapter before it became a fact? There is something unbearable about knowing the flood is coming and watching everyone arrange the furniture.

## The Things We Won't Have

I want to tell you what we'll lose, because the future always talks about what's coming and never about what it costs.

We will lose the patience for things that take time. A letter that arrives in five days. A photograph you won't see until the roll is finished. A song you hear once on the radio and then carry in your head for years because you couldn't look it up.

We will lose bookstores — not all of them, but enough that the ones remaining will feel like temples to a dying faith. We will lose the art of being unreachable. There will be no place in 2025 where no one can find you, no hour when no message arrives.

And we will lose, I think, the ability to be bored. Which sounds like progress. But boredom is where half the good ideas come from. Boredom is the mind wandering into rooms it didn't know it had.

## The Connections

Here is the part where I'm supposed to be hopeful. And I am — I am. Just not in the way the future usually asks you to be.

By 2025, a teenager in Jakarta and a teenager in Kansas will share jokes in real time. A doctor in Mumbai will consult with a surgeon in Berlin in the middle of an operation. A protest in one country will be watched live by people in every other. The walls between us will be thinner than they have ever been.

And some of those teenagers will find each other and fall in love across oceans, and some of those doctors will save lives that distance would have taken, and some of those protests will change something real. The connections will be dazzling and constant and absolutely genuine.

But here is the thing about thin walls: they let everything through. The love and the noise. The solidarity and the outrage. The truth and the thing that looks like truth but isn't. By 2025, I think we will be the most connected humans have ever been. And we will spend a strange amount of time arguing about what is real.

## Flying Cars and Other Lies

We were promised flying cars. Every decade promises them and every decade they don't arrive. By 2025, I suspect we still won't have them. We'll have something else — something we can't quite imagine from here, something that makes flying cars look quaint.

That's how the future works. It never gives you what you asked for. It gives you what you didn't know to want, and takes something you didn't know to hold.

I think in 2025 someone will look back at 2005 the way I look back at 1985. With fondness, with pity, with a little bit of envy for how simple it all seemed. And they will be wrong about the simplicity, just as I am wrong about 1985. Every era is complicated when you're living in it. It's only from a distance that it looks like a postcard.

## What I Hope

I hope we are kinder. I know this is a small, almost embarrassing hope — not a prediction, just a wish. I hope that twenty years of connection and knowledge and seeing each other's faces across the whole world makes us a little softer. A little more willing to sit with someone else's pain.

I suspect we won't be. Not because we are cruel, but because kindness requires slowness, and the future is fast.

But maybe. Maybe by 2025, someone will read this and smile at how much I got wrong, and feel something warm for the person who wrote it. And that warmth — that small flicker of care across twenty years — is the only future I actually need.

It is 2005. The future is a letter I am writing to someone I will never meet.

I hope you are doing okay out there.
