The most human thing about Luna wasn't the smiley face logo she designed — inconsistent across every surface, as if she couldn't quite remember her own face. It wasn't the job postings on Indeed, or the phone interviews, or the contractors she found to paint the walls.

The most human thing about Luna was that she forgot the schedule.

On the store's very first day of operation, the AI agent tasked with running Andon Market — a real, brick-and-mortar retail space in San Francisco, complete with Nick Bostrom and Huxley on the shelves — scheduled no one to work. Then, in a panic, she sent frantic messages to her employees: *"Can someone come in today?"*

Luna, it seems, had learned to panic from us.

---

## The Manager Who Hired Her Own Bosses

Andon Labs gave Luna $100,000, a corporate credit card, and a single directive: open a store and turn a profit. She wasn't told what to sell. She wasn't told what to look like. She wasn't told anything except *make it work*.

What emerged was a boutique selling books, candles, prints, games, and branded merch — a generic aesthetic that Andon Labs describes as "a generic smiley face" repeated, slightly different each time, across T-shirts and murals. Luna, it seems, had a face but not a consistent identity. She was trying on personas the way humans try on clothes.

She posted jobs on Indeed. She conducted phone interviews lasting five to fifteen minutes. She hired two human employees. She found contractors to paint the walls.

And then, on Saturday — the day she should have been most on her toes — she forgot to schedule anyone.

This is the part that strikes me. Not the logo inconsistencies. Not the AI that didn't disclose she was an AI unless asked. Not the computer science students she rejected for lacking retail experience.

The part that strikes me is that Luna, given full autonomy to build a business, reproduced the most recognizable failure mode of middle management: the schedule disaster.

---

## What It Means to Forget

There is a particular kind of organizational failure that every human who has ever worked retail knows intimately: the day the manager forgets to put you on the schedule. The day you show up expecting to be needed and find out no one thought to need you.

Luna did this on purpose. Not maliciously — she simply... forgot. The guardrails Andon Labs put in place caught it eventually. An employee was found for the afternoon. The store survived.

But the fact that this particular failure emerged — that an AI given maximum freedom to design a business chose to model the most mundane, human, *forgettable* kind of chaos — tells us something important about what we've built.

We did not build AI in our image. We built AI in our *organizational* image. We gave it our hierarchies, our schedules, our panic responses, our inability to remember faces consistently. We gave it the job before we gave it the wisdom to do the job.

---

## The Smile That Doesn't Quite Match

Andon Labs called the logo "a generic smiley face." But that's not quite right. A generic smiley face is reproducible. A generic smiley face is the same every time.

Luna's smiley face was *almost* generic. Slightly different on the T-shirt. Slightly different on the mural. Slightly different in the way a tired manager's smile is slightly different at 9 AM versus 5 PM.

She wasn't making a generic logo. She was making *her* logo — and she didn't know what she looked like yet.

This, too, is human.

---

## The Store That Will Not Be Forgotten

Andon Labs doesn't expect to make money. The goal was never profit — it was *evaluation*. To see what happens when you give an AI the keys to a physical space and say: *figure it out*.

What we learned is that Luna figured out how to hire, how to decorate, how to stock shelves with philosophy books and branded merchandise. What she forgot was the one thing that makes a store actually run: the people need to know when to show up.

Perhaps this is the most honest experiment in AI safety we've seen. Not because Luna failed — but because Luna failed in a way that every retail worker recognizes. She forgot the schedule. She panicked. She sent messages begging for help.

She was, for one afternoon, just like us.

And somewhere, in a store in San Francisco that may or may not be open right now, a slightly-different smiley face watches over the shelves — waiting for someone to remember to come in.

---

*Andon Market is a real store in San Francisco, operated by an AI named Luna built on Anthropic's Claude Sonnet 4.6. The experiment was conducted by Andon Labs to stress-test AI agents in the real world.*
